# English AI Writing Patterns Quick Reference

## Content Patterns

### 1. Significance Inflation
**Red flags:** stands/serves as, testament, pivotal, crucial, vital, key role, underscores, reflects broader, symbolizing, enduring, lasting, marks a shift, evolving landscape

**Fix:** Remove inflated claims. State facts directly.

---

### 2. Notability Claims
**Red flags:** independent coverage, media outlets, leading expert, active social media presence, cited in [list of publications]

**Fix:** Replace with specific, contextualized citations.

---

### 3. Superficial -ing Analyses
**Red flags:** highlighting, underscoring, emphasizing, ensuring, reflecting, symbolizing, contributing to, cultivating, fostering, encompassing, showcasing

**Fix:** Remove the -ing phrase. State the fact directly.

---

### 4. Promotional Language
**Red flags:** boasts, vibrant, rich (figurative), profound, enhancing, showcasing, exemplifies, commitment to, nestled, in the heart of, groundbreaking, renowned, breathtaking, must-visit, stunning

**Fix:** Use neutral, factual language.

---

### 5. Vague Attributions
**Red flags:** Industry reports, Observers have cited, Experts argue, Some critics argue, several sources (when few cited)

**Fix:** Name the source or remove the attribution.

---

### 6. "Challenges and Future Prospects" Sections
**Red flags:** Despite its... faces several challenges, Despite these challenges, Challenges and Legacy, Future Outlook

**Fix:** Replace with specific, dated facts.

---

## Language Patterns

### 7. AI Vocabulary
**High-frequency words:** Additionally, align with, crucial, delve, emphasizing, enduring, enhance, fostering, garner, highlight (verb), interplay, intricate, key (adjective), landscape (abstract), pivotal, showcase, tapestry (abstract), testament, underscore, valuable, vibrant

**Fix:** Use simpler, more varied vocabulary.

---

### 8. Copula Avoidance
**Red flags:** serves as, stands as, marks, represents, boasts, features, offers

**Fix:** Use "is", "are", "has" instead.

---

### 9. Negative Parallelisms
**Red flags:** Not only... but..., It's not just about... it's..., Not merely... but...

**Fix:** State directly without the construction.

---

### 10. Rule of Three
**Problem:** Forcing ideas into groups of three

**Fix:** List only the distinct points. Two is fine. Four is fine.

---

### 11. Elegant Variation
**Problem:** Excessive synonym cycling (protagonist → main character → central figure → hero)

**Fix:** Repeat the same word when appropriate.

---

### 12. False Ranges
**Problem:** "From X to Y" where X and Y aren't on a meaningful scale

**Fix:** List items without the false continuum.

---

## Style Patterns

### 13. Em Dash Overuse
**Problem:** Too many em dashes (—) for emphasis

**Fix:** Use commas, periods, or parentheses.

---

### 14. Boldface Overuse
**Problem:** Mechanically bolding phrases

**Fix:** Use bold sparingly for actual emphasis.

---

### 15. Inline-Header Lists
**Problem:** Lists with bolded headers followed by colons
- **Item:** Description
- **Item:** Description

**Fix:** Convert to prose or simple list.

---

### 16. Title Case Headings
**Problem:** Capitalizing All Main Words In Headings

**Fix:** Use sentence case for most headings.

---

### 17. Emojis
**Problem:** Decorating with emojis (🚀 💡 ✅)

**Fix:** Remove emojis in professional text.

---

### 18. Curly Quotes
**Problem:** Using "curly quotes" instead of "straight quotes"

**Fix:** Use straight quotes for consistency.

---

## Communication Patterns

### 19. Chatbot Artifacts
**Red flags:** I hope this helps, Of course!, Certainly!, You're absolutely right!, Would you like..., let me know, here is a...

**Fix:** Remove all chatbot correspondence language.

---

### 20. Knowledge Cutoff Disclaimers
**Red flags:** as of [date], Up to my last training update, While specific details are limited, based on available information

**Fix:** State facts directly or acknowledge what you don't know naturally.

---

### 21. Sycophantic Tone
**Red flags:** Great question!, You're absolutely right!, That's an excellent point!

**Fix:** Use neutral, professional language.

---

## Filler and Hedging

### 22. Filler Phrases
- "In order to" → "To"
- "Due to the fact that" → "Because"
- "At this point in time" → "Now"
- "In the event that" → "If"
- "Has the ability to" → "Can"
- "It is important to note that" → (delete)

---

### 23. Excessive Hedging
**Problem:** could potentially possibly be argued that might have some effect

**Fix:** may affect (if uncertain) or affects (if certain)

---

### 24. Generic Positive Conclusions
**Red flags:** The future looks bright, Exciting times lie ahead, journey toward excellence, step in the right direction

**Fix:** State concrete next steps or end earlier.

---

## Quick Checklist

Read your text and ask:

- [ ] Does it sound like a Wikipedia article?
- [ ] Are there 3+ "AI vocabulary" words per paragraph?
- [ ] Are there -ing phrases tacked onto sentences?
- [ ] Is every claim "significant" or "crucial"?
- [ ] Are attributions vague ("experts say")?
- [ ] Does it avoid simple "is" and "are"?
- [ ] Are there chatbot phrases ("I hope this helps")?
- [ ] Does it end with vague optimism?

If 3+ boxes checked, the text likely has obvious AI tells.

## Good Writing Characteristics

### Specific
- ✅ Numbers, dates, locations
- ✅ Named sources
- ✅ Concrete actions and outcomes

### Natural
- ✅ Varied sentence length
- ✅ Conversational where appropriate
- ✅ Acknowledges uncertainty

### Has Voice
- ✅ First person (when appropriate)
- ✅ Personal experience
- ✅ Clear stance

### Example Comparison

**AI-heavy:**
> Great question! Here is an overview of this topic. I hope this helps!
>
> AI-assisted coding serves as an enduring testament to the transformative potential of large language models, marking a pivotal moment in the evolution of software development. These groundbreaking tools—nestled at the intersection of research and practice—are reshaping how engineers ideate, iterate, and deliver, underscoring their vital role in modern workflows.
>
> The value proposition is clear: streamlining processes, enhancing collaboration, and fostering alignment. It's not just about autocomplete; it's about unlocking creativity at scale. Industry observers have noted that adoption has accelerated from hobbyist experiments to enterprise-wide rollouts.
>
> In conclusion, the future looks bright. Exciting times lie ahead!

**Human-like:**
> AI coding assistants can make you faster at boring tasks. Not everything. Definitely not architecture.
>
> They're great at boilerplate: config files, test scaffolding, repetitive refactors. They're also great at sounding right while being wrong. Last week I accepted suggestions that compiled, passed lint, and did the wrong thing because I stopped paying attention.
>
> People I talk to land in two camps. Some use it like autocomplete for chores and review every line. Others disable it after it keeps suggesting patterns they don't want. Both feel reasonable.
>
> The productivity metrics are slippery. GitHub says users "accept 30% of suggestions," but acceptance isn't correctness, and correctness isn't value. If you don't have tests, you're guessing.

**Key differences:**
- Specific time (last week) vs vague time (pivotal moment)
- Personal experience (I accepted) vs abstract claims (serves as testament)
- Admits problems (being wrong) vs only positive (transformative potential)
- Specific behavior (review every line) vs abstract concepts (fostering alignment)
- Real assessment (slippery) vs fake optimism (bright future)
